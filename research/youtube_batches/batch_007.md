**1. Break-and-retest:**
- The break-and-retest is the core entry model, with the key principle that you wait for a candle closure below/above a level before entering, and the best entries often don't retest deeply. "You have to be a little bit more aggressive because as I mentioned, preferably you like to see sometimes the retest, but the best ones usually don't come back and retesting." (ItlcnXfF1wE_transcript.txt, [4215s-4223s])
- On the downside, entries are often more aggressive and may only offer one opportunity. "If you missed the first one, sometimes there's only one. When you're on the downside, sometimes you have to be a little bit more aggressive. If you're not a little bit more aggressive, you're just gonna be waiting for a trade setup later into the day." (ItlcnXfF1wE_transcript.txt, [4305s-4317s])
- The retest level is often a previous day high/low, pre-market high/low, or a down-close candle (one candle rule). "Tesla retest now is just gonna be that one candle rule, right, that down close candle, that pivot high down close candle. That's gonna be the Tesla, the retest." (J_EFV3bxmzU_transcript.txt, [1695s-1700s])
- There are three entry types: aggressive (limit order at the level), conservative (wait for price action confirmation), and most conservative (wait for a confirmation candle). "there's three different entry types there's the limit there's the strong conservative and then there's the most conservative" (JmOyREca-bM_transcript.txt, [2167s-2173s])
- The break-and-retest is a simple, repeatable strategy, but understanding higher timeframe context is more important than the entry model itself. "But what's more important is understanding where Tesla is most likely going to gravitate towards. If you can understand the drawn liquidity, then technically you can trade any entry model, right? You can have a perfect breaking retest, but if you don't have the correct understanding where the market's going to go, that breaking retest is ultimately going to fail because you don't understand the higher timeframes." (JB61SBv4Syw_transcript.txt, [3024s-3035s])
- A common pattern: break above a level, immediate retest, then continuation. "break above basically a break above this pre-market level right retest this previous day level and then continuation to the upside that was my exact thesis right and that was given at 9 27 a.m. this morning" (IyuUAoSsfnE_transcript.txt, [66s-78s])
- The retest must show strong price action (green close for longs, weak price action/upper wick for shorts). "we need to wait for strong price action. So, as we can see, this was the first candle open right here. We then came down into your previous day high area, and this is where buyers are stepping in with strong price action. This is a green up close candle." (JWRWP9ke2sY_transcript.txt, [345s-368s])
- A stop loss is typically placed just below the entry candle or the key level. "Our stop loss can simply be a break back below your previous day high." (JWRWP9ke2sY_transcript.txt, [388s-397s])
- The 84% rule is a specific type of break-and-retest where you wait for a reclaim of a key level after a false breakdown. "84% of the rules it's a reclaim so it's a reclaim and that's when the best trade occurs right to reclaim of your key level right it's not you could also look for the retest but the retest sometimes doesn't always happen the main thing is the reclaim" (JmOyREca-bM_transcript.txt, [633s-650s])

**3. 84% rule / re-entries:**

- The 84% rule is valid as long as market structure (e.g., higher lows) remains intact. "For the 84% rule, what you always like to see is the market structure still be valid, meaning creating higher lows. Preferably, if it breaks, the best 84% rules occur, breaks underneath it, consolidates, and then sets back up." (JB61SBv4Syw_transcript.txt, [1728s-1746s])
