**1. Break-and-retest:**
- The core system is a break-and-retest methodology with three main entry models: previous day high/low, order block retest, and opening range retest. "the system in itself is a breaking retest and then we have setups within the breaking retest" (_QNeb-S9xv0_transcript.txt, [3856s-3867s])
- The determining factor between an immediate retest (no displacement) and a retest with displacement is context and higher timeframe alignment. "There's a lot of immediate retest that work out really nicely. There's a lot of really good breaking retest with displacement that fail. The determining factor between these two is going to be based off of context and what's going on on the higher time frames." (_QNeb-S9xv0_transcript.txt, [2431s-2443s])
- A classic textbook example: break of previous day high, retest with weak price action (doji/inverted hammer), then continuation to downside targets. "we broke down right below 827.50 which is the previous day close, or previous day high, sorry. We broke below the previous day high. We came back, we retested weak price action, right? Very, very weak price action. And then we closed with this doji candle inverted doji. And so I entered in on the close of this doji looking for a quick low of day move and then 814.82s." (Zvj9wsulyCM_transcript.txt, [122s-145s])
- There are two entry models for a break-and-retest: entry on the pullback into the level (tighter stop, lower risk:reward) or waiting for a close above the level (inversion, higher probability, super tight stop). "If you are looking for a potential break and retest off of, let's say your opening range candle high, right? As it kind of pushes up, comes back down. There's kind of two potential entry models you can look for, right? Number one is your classic, just as soon as it kind of taps into this level, you look for a break and retest back towards highs, right, risking this low. This is like entry model number one, or entry model two is if you wait for a close above this level, right? This is like an inversion, but if you wait for a close above this level, you have a, what's it called? You have a super tighter wrist, but this one's a lot higher probability if it can inverse this level, right?" (_LMcjhKZRss_transcript.txt, [2533s-2572s])
- Waiting for candle closes is critical to avoid being faked out. "A lot of people ask, well, why do you always wait for candle closes? Like it's gonna screw up your risk to reward. It's gonna do this, it's gonna do that, blah, blah, blah. Look, in real time, you guys see why I wait for candle closes. It never closed above the stop. So we stayed in this trade to the scent. It did not close above the stop, right? And then what did it do? It made its way down." (Zvj9wsulyCM_transcript.txt, [336s-355s])
- Aggressive entries (buying a reclaim/breakout) should be lighter size because risk is harder to manage; retest entries allow for tighter stops and clearer targets. "So if you enter on the first one, and it's not a break and retest or it's primarily a little bit more of an aggressive entry, I'll usually go lighter on those aggressive entries because it's harder to manage risk. When let's say you're buying a reclaim, whereas when you're buying a retest, it's very easy to manage risk. You have very tight stop, you know exactly what you're looking for as for targets, whereas when you're buying a reclaim, it's technically you're buying a reclaim, which would be a breakout. So a lot of those times, it's a lot harder to manage risk." (_QNeb-S9xv0_transcript.txt, [1570s-1599s])
- Break-and-retest works after 12 PM, but only on higher timeframes. "Does breaking retest work after 12? It does work after 12, you just have to be on the higher time frames." (_LMcjhKZRss_transcript.txt, [4668s-4672s])
- A large upper wick on a candle makes the break-and-retest above that high less likely to work unless there is a higher timeframe setup. "If you ever see this big of a wick on a candle, the break and retest, right above the high, doesn't usually pan out unless it's like there's a higher timeframe kind of setup involved if you see this big of an upper wick." (_LMcjhKZRss_transcript.txt, [2371s-2380s])

**3. 84% rule / re-entries:**
- The 84% rule states that if a trade comes back to the same level with the same thesis after failing, it should work 84% of the time. "What is the 84% rule? The 84% rule basically states as if a trade comes to your, so for example, we took this first trade, if for the second time the trade comes to that same level with the same thesis, right? It should work in theory, 84% of the time, right?" (Zt8c-5OcLGk_transcript.txt, [194s-213s])
- On the 84% rule re-entry, you can size up because the risk and reward are clearer. "So I took about 60 contracts here, right? So from the first, you guys know how much I took on the first one, right? I about doubled or tripled my size on the second one. Why? This was a better trade for me and the risk was very, very clear and the reward was very, very clear, right?" (Zt8c-5OcLGk_transcript.txt, [241s-254s])
- On the 84% rule re-entry, you do not need to take scales at high of day because there is a higher probability of continuation to the second target. "So we're taking a break and retest setup. A lot of times you're going to take some scales at your, your high day, right, this could be 25% or 50%. But if you have in 84% rule and it resets back up, you don't have to take scales at high day because now there's a better chance that if it does hit high day, now it's going to have continuation towards your second target, which will be TP to." (_QNeb-S9xv0_transcript.txt, [1615s-1659s])
- The 84% rule is only valid if the first trade actually failed (hit its stop) and the same thesis/context still applies. "84% rule, as long as you have the correct 84% because a lot of times what traders will do is they'll prematurely exit without it hitting their stop and they'll enter back in thinking it's an 84% even though the trade, the first trade never failed in itself. So first you have to identify if you're trading a correct 84% rule and if the markets are still intact, if the same thesis still applies with the same context, then that's where your 84% rule comes in." (_QNeb-S9xv0_transcript.txt, [1732s-1755s])

**4. Order blocks:**
- An order block is a down-close candle (in an uptrend) that acts as support. "So where is the order block in, right? It's the up close candle, okay? After a big down close down move, right? So that order block was created right here at 10.07." (_Em7Q3lyrDg_transcript.txt, [96s-105s])
- The full down-close candle (from wick top to wick bottom) is the order block, and the stop is a break below it. "if there's an order block you're going to use it's technically the full downclothes candle" (_QNeb-S9xv0_transcript.txt, [3028s-3032s])
- The highest probability entry within an order block is from the wick to the body. "the highest probabilities is going to be from the wick to the to the sorry the top of the wick to the body" (_QNeb-S9xv0_transcript.txt, [3112s-3119s])
- You want to enter as close to the bottom of the order block as possible to minimize risk. "it's that order block. So it's like, you want to get as close to the stop as possible, right? So you want to get as close to really, the stop is a break below the bottom of the order block. So anywhere in this area in this order block is where you want to be looking to enter, as close to the bottom as the order block as possible." (ZoDEKSAnsiA_transcript.txt, [2654s-2667s])
- A five-minute order block is higher probability than a one-minute order block. "If it was a five minute order block, it would be a lot more high probability than just a one minute order block." (_QNeb-S9xv0_transcript.txt, [1394s-1397s])
- The strongest order blocks are those where price holds the top of the wick and closes above it. "the best order blocks or the best the strongest moves are going to hold the top of the wick right they're going to hold the top of the wick and close above the top of the wick and continue" (_QNeb-S9xv0_transcript.txt, [3071s-3078s])
