## (a) DAY-TRADE DELTAS

**1. "First time touches a key level always has a reaction" — refinement to exit rules**
> "the first time it touches a key level. You always have a reaction. This doesn't mean that it's going to have a full blown reversal... all you know is you're going to have a reversal... so you look to take profits and you look to take partials at those areas"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [634s-671s])

**Delta:** Adds nuance to existing "scale 80-90% at key levels" rule: the *first* touch of a key level guarantees *some* reaction (not necessarily reversal), so partial profits are mandatory there. After the reaction, look for signatures (candle closure above = continuation; rejection/break of structure = reversal) to decide next action.

**2. "Dip first is better than rip first" — refinement to opening play hierarchy**
> "the dip first because we already tap into your higher time frame objective... we see that buyers are stepping in... and then it basically breaks back above your opening range candle... this is one of the most bullish setups"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [2234s-2284s])

**Delta:** Adds a preference hierarchy to existing opening play rules: dip-first (into HTF level) + reclaim of opening range > break-and-retest of pre-market high. The dip shows buyer strength at the HTF level; the reclaim confirms intent. Rip-first (no dip) is riskier because you don't know if buyers are stepping in.

**3. "Premarket highs/lows are minor — only mark when there's volume" — refinement to key levels**
> "I really don't put too much emphasis on pre-market highs and pre-market lows... the only time I mark out premarket levels is when there's actually volume... if we're just inside the middle of the range... these premarket levels are not really that useful to me"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [2458s-3143s])

**Delta:** Contradicts the general assumption that premarket highs/lows are always key levels. They are only relevant when there's volume (e.g., breaking above previous day highs). Otherwise, they are minor and should not be used as standalone retest levels.

**4. "Three entry models at market open" — new framework for opening plays**
> "number one is just buying the dip... number two is going to be your reclaim... number three is going to be your opening range break and retest"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [1254s-1306s])

**Delta:** Formalizes three distinct entry models for opening plays (dip-buy, reclaim, break-and-retest) that were previously implicit. The reclaim entry (buying when price closes back above opening range) is presented as a valid alternative when the retest doesn't happen.

**5. "Reclaim entry has clearer risk management than dip-buy" — refinement to risk management**
> "if you buy the dip... your risk is unknown... if you buy off the reclaim then you can risk off this low because this is a pivot low that buyers have stepped in... it's a lot easier to manage risk"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [2761s-2820s])

**Delta:** Adds a risk-management rationale for preferring reclaim entries over dip-buys: the reclaim provides a clear, objective stop level (the pivot low), whereas dip-buying is more discretionary with no fixed risk reference.

**6. "Context matters more than pattern — opening range break alone is ~50% win rate" — refinement to entry model**
> "if you just strictly just trade five minute opening range break... you'll probably have around a 50% win rate... but if you want to increase your win rate... you can include developing a thesis... understanding what's going on the higher time frames"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [1464s-1529s])

**Delta:** Quantifies the baseline win rate of the mechanical opening range break pattern (~50%) and explicitly states that HTF context is what elevates it to A+ trades. This contradicts any assumption that the pattern alone is high-probability.

**7. "If you can't gauge HTF direction, don't trade lower timeframes" — new gate rule**
> "if you can't consistently go into the higher time frames every single day and be like, okay, I think price is going to gravitate here... then you should probably not be on the lower time frames"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [1547s-1560s])

**Delta:** Adds a hard gate: inability to form a daily HTF thesis disqualifies you from intraday trading. This is a stricter version of the existing "HTF thesis first" rule.

**8. "Spy/QQQ mostly consolidate — tech names for continuation" — refinement to instrument selection**
> "majority of the times spy and q's does not have continuation... it usually rejects off previous highs and previous lows... that's why I do trade tech names because there's always usually some sort of higher time frame setup"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [1627s-1655s])

**Delta:** Adds a structural preference: for continuation trades (break-and-retest), favor individual tech names over indices because indices are mostly range-bound. Indices are better for gap-fills and reversal trades.

**9. "Zones vs levels — multiple touches create zones" — refinement to level drawing**
> "if there's multiple touches... I'll draw this whole level... it'll be a lot wider range... because sometimes you'll see price... will tap the top or sometimes dig a little bit deeper into the bottom of the zone"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [1884s-1942s])

**Delta:** Adds a distinction: single-touch levels (e.g., previous day high) are drawn as lines; multi-touch areas become zones with width. This refines the existing "key levels" rule by adding a zone-drawing methodology.

**10. "Order blocks need multiple confluences to be relevant" — refinement to order block usage**
> "majority of the times you want to look for ones that have multiple touches... coincides with key levels and other key break and retest levels as well"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [3071s-3075s])

**Delta:** Adds a relevance filter: a single down-close candle (order block) is not sufficient; it needs multiple touches and confluence with other key levels to be tradable.

## (b) SWING / LONG-TERM

**1. Swing trading = same setup on higher timeframe**
> "It's the basically the exact same thing but on the higher time frames... you would wait for a break retest continuation towards upside... it's basically the exact same thing on the lower time frames but on a higher time frame"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [3158s-3201s])

**2. Swing trading requires more room on contracts (options)**
> "when you're swing trading... you got to give it a little bit more room on the contracts, especially for trading options"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [3206s-3211s])

**3. Swing trading advantage: lets the trade develop, reduces overtrading**
> "swing trading... it's good because you let the trade actually develop... when you're swing trading you actually let it develop and you actually let the trade you see what's going on the higher time... whereas if you're intraday trading you can flip back and forward too much"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [3224s-3265s])

**4. Swing trading = clear thesis, clear risk level, wait for development**
> "when you're swing trading you have a clear thesis in play you have clear risk level... you just wait and let it develop"
(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [3253s-3260s])

**5. Example: Tesla swing setup — break above 222/223 for continuation**

**6. Pre-earnings chop — don't trade inside range before catalyst**

(FF4SsAn19go_transcript.txt, [3377s-3399s])

**7. Swing trading = accept risk either way (same as intraday)**

**8. HTF consolidation = low probability for swing entries**
